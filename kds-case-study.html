Kohl's Design System

Reviving the Kohl's Design System

For more than 2 years, I was the primary custodian and contributor to the Kohl's Design System. Getting the system up to enterprise-standards was a tall order, but I was up to the task.

The Problem

When I was assigned to Kohl’s Design System (KDS), it had been defunct and abandoned for over two years. Although designers were expected to use it, there was no clear guidance or documentation. The Figma components were rigid, difficult to use, and the underlying codebase was outdated, making adoption frustrating and inconsistent across teams.

The Process

Tasked with revitalizing KDS and driving adoption, I partnered closely with the engineering team to update the codebase and ensure technical stability. I actively solicited feedback from design teams through weekly critiques to understand their needs, iterating on components until they were truly useful and easy to use. Overcoming a lack of leadership direction, I focused on making the design system better by empowering designers rather than enforcing usage.

The Solutions

I redesigned and documented scalable Figma components tailored to real design workflows, including complex components like the product tile—crafted to handle every variation through modular subcomponents. I also implemented a feedback and requests form directly tied to our backlog, streamlining communication and saving time for both contributors and maintainers.

Results & Impact

While precise metrics were hard to track, one metric I am proud of is that by the time I was finished, adoption reached 100%. When I started, adoption was only at 12%. Our Site Standards team could work more efficiently, thanks to alignment between updated KDS styles and the evolving site pages. The product tile component became a shared resource, adopted by multiple designers and featured in high-visibility patterns like product carousels and homepage recommendations.

Reflection

This project taught me the power of resourcefulness in the face of unclear authority and limited support. By focusing on usability and collaboration, I was able to inspire team members to care about and contribute to the system’s growth. Tackling the most complex component first—as advised by design system expert Dan Mall—helped pave the way for smoother development of all subsequent components, turning what felt like an uphill battle into meaningful progress.
